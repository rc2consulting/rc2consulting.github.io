🏛️ U.S. Small Business Administration (SBA) — Complete Guide

The SBA is a federal agency created to support American small businesses through financing, contracting assistance, and certifications. Understanding SBA programs is critical for RC2 clients who want to access government contracts, funding, and competitive advantages available only to small businesses.

📏
What is a "Small Business"?
The SBA defines "small" by industry using NAICS codes. Size standards are based either on number of employees or average annual receipts (revenue) over the past 3 years. Most manufacturing businesses qualify at 500–1,500 employees. Most service businesses qualify at $8–$47 million in revenue.
🔢
What is a NAICS Code?
North American Industry Classification System (NAICS) codes are 6-digit numbers that classify businesses by the type of work they do. Every government contract has a NAICS code. Your client's NAICS code determines their SBA size standard and which set-aside programs they are eligible for.
🏆
Why SBA Certifications Matter
Federal agencies are required to award 23% of all prime contract dollars to small businesses. Certain certifications (8(a), WOSB, HUBZone, SDVOSB) allow companies to compete in restricted pools — dramatically reducing competition and increasing win probability.
💰
SBA Financing Programs
The SBA offers loan guarantee programs (7(a), 504, Microloans) that give lenders confidence to extend credit to small businesses that might not otherwise qualify. SBA does not lend directly — it guarantees a portion of the loan made by an approved lender.
🎯
Government Contract Set-Asides
Set-aside contracts are reserved exclusively for certain business categories. When a federal contract is "set aside" for small businesses, only certified small businesses may bid. This eliminates competition from large corporations and can be a major competitive advantage for your clients.
⚠️
Common Mistakes RC2 Sees
Clients register with the wrong NAICS code, miss certification opportunities because they don't know they qualify, fail to maintain their SAM.gov registration (expires annually), and don't understand that size standards apply per contract, not per company.
📋
SAM.gov — The Starting Point
System for Award Management (SAM.gov) is the federal registration required for all government contractors. Must be renewed annually. Without an active SAM.gov registration, a company cannot receive any federal contract award, regardless of certifications.

📌 How to Find the Right NAICS Code for Your Client
Step 1: Identify Primary Activity
The NAICS code must reflect what the business primarily does — where most revenue comes from. A company that manufactures AND sells uses a manufacturing code, not retail.
Step 2: Search Multiple Keywords
Try several descriptive keywords. NAICS descriptions don't always use everyday language. "Apparel contractor" and "cut and sew" both describe NAICS 315.
Step 3: A Company Can Have Multiple Codes
Businesses register a primary code in SAM.gov but can list secondary codes. For contracting, the specific solicitation's NAICS code determines the applicable size standard.
Step 4: Verify with SBA Table
Always verify the size standard at sba.gov/size-standards. The SBA updates size standards regularly. Never rely on outdated tables for compliance purposes.
📏 SBA Size Standards Calculator
The SBA size standard determines whether your client qualifies as a "small business" for a specific federal contract. Size is measured differently by industry — some use annual revenue (average of last 3 years), others use number of employees (average of last 12 months, including all affiliates).
📋 Key Size Standard Rules Every RC2 Consultant Must Know
Affiliation Rules
The SBA counts affiliated businesses toward your size. Affiliation exists when one company controls another, they share common ownership, or they are economically dependent. This is the most common reason companies are found "other than small."
Average vs. Snapshot
Revenue is averaged over the last 3 fiscal years. Employees are the average over the last 12 months. A company that grew rapidly may still qualify based on 3-year average revenue — and vice versa.
Size Is Determined at Time of Offer
For most contracts, size is determined at the time the offer (bid) is submitted. A company that grows beyond the size standard after award can typically complete that contract without penalty.
Manufacturing vs. Services
Manufacturing NAICS codes use employee counts (500–1,500 employees). Service, construction, and most other industries use revenue-based standards ($8M–$47M). Wholesale trade uses a 100-employee or 250-employee standard.

🏆 SBA Certifications — Complete Guide

SBA certifications unlock access to federal set-aside contracts reserved for specific business categories. Federal agencies are legally mandated to award percentage targets to certified businesses. Getting the right certifications can transform a client's revenue potential.

8️⃣
8(a) Business Development Program
SBA — Certify.sba.gov
9-Year Program
The 8(a) program helps small disadvantaged businesses compete for federal contracts. Participants receive mentorship, training, and the ability to receive sole-source contracts up to $4.5M (services) or $7M (manufacturing). This is the most powerful SBA certification.
✅ Sole-source contracts up to $7M (manufacturing)
✅ Access to $90B+ in annual set-aside spending
✅ Mentor-protégé program access
✅ Business development training
Key Requirements
Socially and economically disadvantaged individual must own 51%+
Owner must be U.S. citizen and involved in daily management
Owner's personal net worth ≤ $850,000 (excluding primary home and business equity)
Owner's adjusted gross income ≤ $400,000 (3-yr average)
Business must be small per SBA size standards
Business must have been operating for 2 years (exceptions exist)
👩
Women-Owned Small Business (WOSB)
SBA — Certify.sba.gov
Women-Owned
The WOSB program enables women-owned small businesses to compete for federal contracts in industries where women are underrepresented. EDWOSB (economically disadvantaged) allows additional advantages similar to 8(a).
✅ Access to WOSB set-aside contracts
✅ Sole-source contracts up to $4.5M (services) / $7M (manufacturing)
✅ EDWOSB designation for additional advantages
Key Requirements
Women own 51%+ of the business
Women must manage day-to-day operations and long-term decisions
Owner must be U.S. citizen
Business must be small per SBA size standards
For EDWOSB: personal net worth ≤ $850,000
🗺️
HUBZone Program
SBA — Certify.sba.gov
Location-Based
HUBZone (Historically Underutilized Business Zone) certification is for businesses located in and primarily employing workers from designated economically distressed areas. Federal agencies must award 3% of contract dollars to HUBZone businesses.
✅ 10% price evaluation preference over non-HUBZone companies
✅ Set-aside and sole-source contracts
✅ 3% of all federal contract spending target
Key Requirements
Business principal office in a designated HUBZone
51%+ owned by U.S. citizens, Community Development Corporations, agricultural cooperatives, Alaska Native Corporations, or Indian tribal governments
At least 35% of employees must reside in a HUBZone
Business must be small per SBA size standards
🎖️
Service-Disabled Veteran-Owned (SDVOSB)
SBA — Certify.sba.gov (also VA VBOC)
Veteran-Owned
The SDVOSB program helps service-disabled veterans compete for federal contracts. Veterans Affairs (VA) has its own verification program (VOSB/SDVOSB) for VA-specific contracts. Federal agencies must award 3% of contract dollars to SDVOSB firms.
✅ Set-aside and sole-source contracts
✅ Strong preference in VA contracting
✅ 3% of federal contract spending target
✅ Mentor-protégé program access
Key Requirements
51%+ owned by one or more service-disabled veterans
Veteran must have service-connected disability rating from VA
Veteran must control and manage daily operations
Business must be small per SBA size standards
🏢
Small Disadvantaged Business (SDB)
SBA — SAM.gov self-certification
Self-Certified
SDB status is a self-certification that a company is at least 51% owned by economically and socially disadvantaged individuals. It is different from the 8(a) program. SDB status can be used by large prime contractors to meet subcontracting goals.
✅ Counted toward large contractor SDB subcontracting goals
✅ May qualify for certain agency-specific preferences
✅ Foundation for other certifications
Key Requirements
51%+ owned by socially and economically disadvantaged individuals
Self-certification through SAM.gov profile
Business must be small per SBA size standards
🌐
SAM.gov Registration
sam.gov — Required for ALL federal contractors
Mandatory
SAM.gov (System for Award Management) is NOT a certification — it is a mandatory federal registration required to receive any federal contract, grant, or payment. Without an active SAM.gov registration, a business cannot be awarded a federal contract regardless of any certifications.
✅ Required for all federal contracting
✅ Must be renewed annually
✅ Lists all NAICS codes, certifications, and banking info
✅ Failure to renew = cannot receive federal payments
Key Requirements
Active registration on SAM.gov (annual renewal)
Valid DUNS/UEI number
Current and accurate business information including NAICS codes
Banking information for direct deposit of federal payments

The 7(a) is the SBA's primary loan program. It can be used for working capital, equipment, real estate, refinancing existing debt, or purchasing a business. Sole Source Contracts
Certain certified businesses (8(a), SDVOSB, WOSB) can receive contracts without competition. This is the most powerful advantage in government contracting. Sole-source contracts are awarded without a competitive bid process when the contracting officer determines it is in the government's interest.
📋
Total Small Business Set-Asides
When only small businesses will be competing for a contract, the contracting officer issues a "total small business set-aside." Any small business (without specific certification) can bid. The "rule of two" applies: if two or more small businesses are likely to submit offers, the contract must be set aside.
🤝
Subcontracting Plans
Large prime contractors on federal contracts above $750,000 (construction: $1.5M) must have a subcontracting plan with goals for small business subcontracting. This creates a significant pipeline of opportunities for small businesses who do not want to be prime contractors.
🔍
Where to Find Set-Aside Contracts
SAM.gov/contracts lists all federal contract opportunities. Filter by "set-aside type" to find opportunities matching your client's certifications. Also check agency-specific forecast pages and FedBizOpps (now integrated into SAM.gov).
🏭
GSA Schedules (MAS)
The GSA Multiple Award Schedule (MAS) is a long-term contract vehicle that pre-approves companies to sell to federal agencies at pre-negotiated rates. Once on GSA Schedule, agencies can purchase directly without a competitive bid. There are separate tracks for small businesses and socioeconomic categories.
⚠️
Set-Aside Compliance Rules
Set-aside contracts come with performance requirements. For example, a small business receiving a set-aside manufacturing contract must perform at least 50% of the work itself (cannot subcontract more than 50% to a non-small business). Violations can result in contract termination and debarment.
