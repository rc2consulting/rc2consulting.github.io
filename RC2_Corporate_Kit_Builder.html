🗂️ What Is a Corporate Kit — and Why Does It Matter?

A corporate kit is the complete collection of foundational legal documents and records that establish, govern, and protect a business entity. Every properly formed business — LLC, Corporation, or S-Corp — must have these documents in place. RC2 helps clients build and maintain theirs from day one.

LLC · C-Corp · S-Corp · Nonprofit Legally Required in Most States Protects Owner's Personal Assets Required for Banking, Contracts & Financing
⚖️
Why It's Non-Negotiable
Without proper formation documents, courts may "pierce the corporate veil" — meaning the owner is personally liable for business debts and lawsuits. A properly maintained corporate kit is the primary legal defense separating the owner's personal assets from the business.
🏦
Banks & Lenders Require It
Any bank opening a business account, any lender providing a business loan, and any SBA program requires proof of entity formation. They will ask for Articles of Organization/Incorporation, EIN letter, and operating agreement or bylaws before processing anything.
📋
What's Inside a Corporate Kit
Articles of Incorporation or Organization, Bylaws (Corp) or Operating Agreement (LLC), EIN confirmation letter, initial meeting minutes, officer/director resolutions, banking resolutions, membership certificates or stock certificates, and the annual compliance calendar.
🏛️
LLC vs. Corporation — Key Differences
An LLC uses an Operating Agreement and has Members/Managers. A Corporation uses Bylaws and has Officers, Directors, and Shareholders. Both need Articles filed with the state, an EIN from the IRS, and internal governance documents. S-Corp is a tax election made on top of a Corporation or LLC.
📅
Ongoing Compliance Requirements
Formation is not a one-time event. Most states require Annual Reports or Statements of Information filed annually with a fee. Corporations must hold annual meetings and document them. Officers and registered agents must be kept current. Failure to comply can result in dissolution by the state.
🔑
Registered Agent
Every entity must have a registered agent — a person or service authorized to receive legal documents on behalf of the company in the state of formation. This can be the owner, an attorney, or a registered agent service (e.g., Northwest, Incfile). The agent must be available during business hours at a physical address.
🔢
EIN — Employer Identification Number
The EIN is the business's federal tax ID, issued by the IRS (apply free at IRS.gov — takes 5 minutes online). Required for opening a bank account, hiring employees, filing taxes, applying for licenses, and most government programs. There is no excuse for not having one.

💡 Officer Title Reference Guide
CEO / President
Chief Executive Officer or President — runs the company day-to-day. Highest executive authority. Signs major contracts and legal documents.
CFO / Treasurer
Chief Financial Officer or Treasurer — oversees all financial matters, banking, and financial reporting. Signs checks above a threshold.
Secretary
Maintains corporate records, meeting minutes, and filings. Signs certain legal documents. Required role in most states for corporations.
Managing Member (LLC)
For member-managed LLCs: all members have authority. For manager-managed LLCs: only designated managers can act on the company's behalf.
Director
Board member responsible for governance and major strategic decisions. Directors are elected by shareholders. They do not run day-to-day operations.
Registered Agent
The designated recipient for legal and government notices. Not an officer — but a required role for the entity. Must have a physical address in the state.
